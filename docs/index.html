Mapping Florida's blackout

A county-by-county reconstruction of the power outages caused by Hurricane Ian, September 2022.

By Chukwudi Odoh  ·  Geospatial data analysis
Data: ORNL EAGLE-I · U.S. Census · Mapped in QGIS

At its peak, Hurricane Ian left 2,500,687 customers without power across Florida — a number reached at 10 p.m. on 29 September 2022, as the storm tore inland from the Gulf coast. This page reconstructs that blackout the way the grid actually experienced it: county by county, every fifteen minutes, from the moment the first lights flickered to the last reconnection.

The reconstruction draws on the U.S. Department of Energy's EAGLE-I system, which logs outage counts for utilities nationwide. I pulled the gated 2014–2022 archive, narrowed it to Florida and the storm window, and rebuilt the per-county picture from the raw readings — then mapped the result on an equal-area projection in QGIS. Nothing here is illustrative; every figure is computed from the source data and regenerates from a single script.

The map needs little explanation, because it draws itself. Ian came ashore in southwest Florida on 28 September, and the single darkest county sits exactly there. From the landfall zone a band of red runs north-east through the central peninsula, tracing the storm's path across the state, while the panhandle and far north — which Ian never reached — stay pale. That spatial signature was never specified; it fell out of the data because it is true.

The statewide peak — just over 2.5 million — lands close to the figure of roughly 2.7 million widely reported for Ian. The small gap is expected: EAGLE-I covers most, but not all, U.S. utility customers. That the two agree is the point. It is the cross-check that lets the per-county numbers be trusted, rather than merely plotted.

How it was built

A deterministic, scripted pipeline. No figure is hand-entered, and one command reproduces the whole thing.

  1. AcquirePull the ORNL EAGLE-I 2014–2022 outage archive (~1 GB, gated) through Globus, with U.S. Census county boundaries.
  2. SliceChunk-filter to Florida and the 26 Sep – 10 Oct window; normalise county FIPS codes — leaving 60,559 readings.
  3. PeakCompute each county's maximum customers-out, and the statewide peak with its exact timestamp.
  4. SeverityIntegrate customers-out over time into customer-hours per county — duration-weighted impact, not just the worst moment.
  5. RestoreBuild the statewide restoration curve and per-county restoration times.
  6. ValidateHard checks — FIPS coverage, timestamp ordering, non-negative counts — must pass before anything is written.
  7. MapJoin the peak table to county polygons, write a GeoPackage, and render the equal-area choropleth in QGIS.

What it took

The skills the project actually exercised, end to end.

Spatial analysis & cartography
Choropleth design in QGIS, equal-area projection choice, natural-breaks classification, and legible handling of zero versus no-data.
Geospatial Python
GeoPandas, Shapely, pyproj and pyogrio for the join, reprojection and GeoPackage output; NumPy for the customer-hours integration.
Data engineering at scale
Chunked filtering of a gigabyte-plus archive, deterministic transforms, and outputs that regenerate from one script.
Validation & reproducibility
Built-in quality gates and re-derivation of every figure from source — fully auditable, nothing typed by hand.
